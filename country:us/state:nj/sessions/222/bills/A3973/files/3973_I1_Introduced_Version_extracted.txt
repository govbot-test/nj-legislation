Specifically, the bill would exempt the 혫intra-utility transportation or processing혬 of soil and fill recyclable materials from the provisions of the Dirty Dirt law.혻 Under the bill, the 혫intra-utility transportation or processing혬 of soil and fill recyclable materials would include cases where a public utility, or a utility contractor, generates the materials at a utility facility and the materials are transported by a public utility or utility contractor to a utility facility that accepts soil, fill, and solid waste managed by the public utility or its utility contractor for temporary storage, characterization, and other management prior to final disposal or recycling.혻 The bill would establish certain requirements, in the definition of 혫intra-utility transportation or processing,혬 in order for an activity to be considered exempt. The bill would also amend the definition of 혫key employee혬 in the A-901 law to exclude brokers, consultants, and salespeople employed by, or who do business with, an applicant for an A-901 license.혻 Under current law, these persons are considered 혫key employees혬 and are thus required to undergo fingerprinting and complete a personal history disclosure form.혻 This bill would remove these requirements. The bill would also amend the definition of 혫broker혬 in the A-901 law to clarify that a person who, on the person혪s own behalf, retains the services of a licensed business concern to provide for the collection, transportation, treatment, storage, processing, transfer, or disposal of solid waste or hazardous waste, or the provision of soil and fill recycling services, at a property, would not be considered a broker by the Department of Environmental Protection (DEP). In addition, the bill would modify the definition of 혫soil and fill recyclable materials혬 to modify the types of soil that would be included in the definition.혻 For example, the bill would exclude alternative fill material approved for use at a site under the direction and oversight of a licensed site remediation professional (LSRP). The bill would also exclude, from the definition of 혫soil and fill recycling services,혬 the excavation, transportation, placement, or processing of soil and fill recyclable materials under the oversight of a LSRP. The bill would also exempt the purchase and sale of soil and fill recyclable materials from the provisions the Dirty Dirt law, meaning that only persons involved in the collection, transportation, processing, brokering, storage, or disposition of soil and fill recyclable materials would be covered under that law.혻 The bill would also establish a de minimis exemption from the provisions of the Dirty Dirt law, namely for persons that transport less than 200 cubic yards of soil and fill recyclable materials to or from a single site, or store less than 200 cubic yards of soil and fill recyclable materials at a single site.혻 Finally, the bill would require persons covered under the Dirty Dirt law to submit an application for a soil and fill recycling license within 45 days, rather than 30 days as in current law, after the DEP adopts rules and regulations to implement the Dirty Dirt law.

STATEMENT This bill would modify certain provisions concerning the licenses issued under P.L.1983, c.392 (C.13:1E-126 et seq.), colloquially known as the 혫A-901혬 law, and P.L.2019, c.397 (C.13:1E-127.1 et al.), colloquially known as the 혫Dirty Dirt혬 law. The bill would also amend the definition of 혫key employee혬 in the A-901 law to exclude brokers, consultants, and salespeople employed by, or who do business with, an applicant for an A-901 license.혻 Under current law, these persons are considered 혫key employees혬 and are thus required to undergo fingerprinting and complete a personal history disclosure form.혻 This bill would remove these requirements.
